MUSE Cell Infusion Linked to Improved Motor Function at 12 Weeks in Subacute Stroke Cohort

In a Phase II cohort of 58 patients treated 10–30 days after ischemic stroke, a single intravenous dose of MUSE cells was associated with meaningful gains on standardized motor scales, researchers reported — though the study was not designed to prove efficacy.

By Elena Ruiz, PhD
Boston, Mass. · Jul 19, 2026 · 6 min read
Illustration: MUSE cells are theorized to sense sphingosine-1-phosphate signals released by damaged tissue and home there after IV delivery. Credit: Regenera Graphics.

A Phase II study of an intravenously delivered MUSE cell product reported improved motor function scores among stroke patients treated within a month of their event, according to results summarized this week from a multi-site academic consortium. The open-label cohort of 58 participants showed a mean improvement on the Fugl-Meyer motor assessment that investigators described as "clinically meaningful" relative to a matched historical comparison group, though the trial lacked a concurrent placebo arm.

The therapy, delivered as a single outpatient infusion roughly two to four weeks after stroke onset, required no immunosuppression and no surgical delivery — a departure from many earlier cell-therapy approaches for stroke, which relied on direct intracerebral injection. Researchers say that simplicity is central to MUSE cells' appeal as a scalable treatment.

How the cells are thought to work

MUSE — Multilineage-differentiating Stress Enduring — cells are a small, naturally occurring population of stress-tolerant stem cells first identified within adult mesenchymal tissue. Unlike induced pluripotent stem cells, they are collected and expanded without genetic reprogramming, and preclinical work to date has not shown them to form tumors. After intravenous infusion, MUSE cells appear to sense signals released by damaged tissue — including sphingosine-1-phosphate — and preferentially migrate, or "home," to the site of injury, where they are thought to differentiate and support repair through both direct and paracrine mechanisms.

In this cohort, imaging substudies using labeled-cell tracking in a subset of patients showed accumulation of signal in peri-infarct brain tissue within 72 hours of infusion, consistent with that homing hypothesis, according to the researchers' summary of the data.

"The most striking observation wasn't a single dramatic recovery — it was the consistency of small gains across a fairly diverse patient population." — Marcus Ohene, MD, PhD, the trial's principal investigator

What comes next

The investigators caution against overinterpreting an open-label, non-randomized dataset, and note that spontaneous recovery in the weeks following stroke is well documented and could account for some of the observed improvement. A larger, randomized, sham-controlled Phase III trial is now being designed, with enrollment expected to begin at additional academic medical centers in the coming months.

Regulatory observers say a positive Phase III readout would represent one of the more significant developments yet for cell-based stroke therapy — a field that has seen repeated early promise followed by mixed confirmatory results. For now, the therapy remains investigational and is available only through registered clinical trials.

By Elena Ruiz, PhD·4 hr ago·6 min read

Explainer

What are MUSE cells?

MUSE cells were first described as a stress-tolerant subpopulation within mesenchymal-lineage cells, identifiable by the surface marker SSEA-3. They combine some pluripotent-like differentiation capacity with a strong non-tumorigenic safety profile.

  • Discovered within bone marrow, adipose, and connective tissue
  • Survive stress conditions that eliminate most other cell types
  • Home to injury sites via sphingosine-1-phosphate signaling
  • Delivered by simple IV infusion — no cell-matching required for some products
  • Under study for stroke, AMI, ALS, and epidermolysis bullosa
