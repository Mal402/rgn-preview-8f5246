A nameplate of its own

Every great broadsheet keeps two typefaces on staff. One is a workhorse — set small, read for minutes at a time, invisible by design. The other appears once per page, at enormous size, and has exactly one job: to be recognized instantly and never mistaken for anyone else's paper.

The New York Times, the Wall Street Journal, the Guardian — none of them set their nameplate in the same face that carries their reporting. The masthead is a small, deliberate piece of type design, commissioned and tuned on its own terms. The body copy underneath is a different animal entirely: quiet, legible, unglamorous, built to disappear into the reading experience.

Regenera Newswire's problem is that both jobs are currently being done by the same typeface. Georgia is an excellent workhorse — which is exactly why it should stay exactly where it is, in the article body, and nowhere else. What follows are five candidate voices for the one job Georgia was never meant to do: standing alone, at large size, as the thing readers recognize before they've read a single word.

For reference — body text is Georgia, unchanged

Trial data show durable engraftment at 18 monthsInvestigators reported Tuesday that early-phase results across three regenerative-medicine cohorts continue to show favorable safety signals, adding to a growing body of evidence for MUSE-cell-derived therapies in degenerative conditions. This paragraph — like every article on the site — is set in Georgia. Nothing below changes that.

The strongest concept, featured throughout

Cell + Signal — a node emitting a broadcast pulse. It marries Regenera (the cell, the biology) with Newswire (the signal, the wire). It appears here as the throughline, and again as the primary mark in Direction 03.
